The Long-Term Psychological Effects of Eating Disorders - Eating disorders are not just about food or body weight—they are mental health conditions with serious psychological consequences. If left untreated, these disorders can lead to ongoing mental health issues, such as anxiety, depression, and obsessive-compulsive behaviors. They can also lead to social isolation and increase the risk of suicidal ideation.

Anxiety and Depression - Eating disorders and anxiety or depression often coexist, creating a cycle of distress that can be challenging to break. The constant preoccupation with food, weight, and body image can heighten feelings of anxiety and hopelessness, leading to a depressive state. In fact, our team at Core Recovery has found that many individuals struggling with eating disorders also experience symptoms of depression and anxiety.

Obsessive Thoughts and Compulsive Behaviors - Individuals with eating disorders may frequently battle obsessive thoughts related to food and their bodies. These obsessions can disrupt daily life, making it difficult to focus on anything else. Compulsive behaviors, such as repeated weighing or excessive exercise, may also develop as a way to control weight and food intake. These obsessive thoughts and compulsive actions can exacerbate the eating disorder and contribute to a cycle of worsening mental health.

Social Isolation and Relationship Disruptions - Many people with eating disorders isolate themselves due to feelings of shame or guilt associated with their condition. This isolation can hinder recovery and exacerbate mental health issues. They may also experience disruptions in their relationships, as the disorder can strain friendships and family ties, leading to further isolation and emotional distress.

Increased Risk of Suicidal Ideation - The mental toll of eating disorders can also increase the risk of suicidal ideation. The feelings of despair, hopelessness, and isolation associated with these disorders can lead to thoughts of self-harm or suicide. It’s crucial to recognize these risks and seek professional help immediately if you or someone you know displays any signs of suicidal thoughts.

The Specific Long-Term Effects of Different Eating Disorders - Eating disorders are not monolithic; each type has distinct characteristics and can result in unique long-term effects. Let’s delve into the specific complications associated with Anorexia Nervosa, Bulimia Nervosa, and Binge Eating Disorder.

Anorexia Nervosa: From Organ Damage to Mental Health Problems - Anorexia Nervosa (AN) is an eating disorder characterized by extreme weight loss and self-starvation. It carries serious health risks, and the long-term effects can be both physically and mentally devastating.

Physically, AN can lead to severe malnutrition, resulting in organ damage. This includes the heart, where the body begins to break down muscle tissue when deprived of essential nutrients, leading to cardiac problems and even sudden cardiac death. Another significant issue is a reduction in bone density, which can lead to osteoporosis.

The reproductive system is also affected, with hormonal changes leading to irregular or absent periods in those who menstruate. In some cases, AN can lead to an irreversible loss of fertility.

Mental health problems are also common. The fear of gaining weight and obsessive thoughts about food can intensify over time and co-occur with other mental health conditions, such as anxiety and depression.

Bulimia Nervosa: Dental Issues, Esophageal Damage, and Heart Problems

Bulimia Nervosa involves cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors like purging, fasting, or excessive exercise. These behaviors can have severe consequences on the body over time.

Physical complications can include dental problems due to recurrent vomiting, which exposes the teeth to stomach acid, causing tooth decay. The frequent purging can also lead to esophageal damage and gastrointestinal issues like acid reflux and gastritis.

The strain of these behaviors on the body can result in cardiovascular complications, such as irregular heart rhythms, low blood pressure, and heart palpitations. In severe cases, these can escalate to heart failure.

Binge Eating Disorder: Weight Gain, High Blood Pressure, and Increased Risk of Cancer

Binge Eating Disorder (BED) is characterized by periods of uncontrolled, impulsive, or continuous eating beyond the point of feeling comfortably full. Unlike bulimia, there are no regular attempts to “make up” for the binges through vomiting, fasting, or over-exercising.

People with BED can experience rapid weight gain, which can lead to obesity. This in turn increases the risk of medical complications like high blood pressure, heart disease, Type II diabetes, and certain types of cancer.

BED also has psychological effects. The guilt and shame associated with bingeing can lead to further episodes, creating a vicious cycle that can be hard to break without professional help.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I don't have insurance or you don't take my insurance?

We accept most insurance policies and have cash pay options that are very reasonable. During your initial admissions call we will be able to discuss your insurance and/or cash pay schedule. When you are ready to proceed your admissions coordinator will verify insurance coverage for you.

Can I keep my Psychiatrist while I am in your program?

We ask that you put all other provider services on hold except for your primary care physician while you are receiving services here at Core Recovery. Our team of knowledgeable, caring professionals, including psychiatric nurse practitioners, clinicians, case managers and medical assistants will develop a customized treatment program to provide you with the best possible care.

Why or when is an IOP or PHP better than just seeing an individual therapist once or twice per week?

If you are just coming out of a hospital or residential treatment center, or you are finding that the progress you would like to see with individual therapy is just not there, it may be time to consider an Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP) or Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP). Attending at this level of care provides more structured support than individual therapy alone.

Individual therapy is a great start; however, if you believe you would benefit from a program that offers much more support such as individual, group, family, and art therapy and psychiatric care (medication management) all in-house, then Core is right for you. IOP and PHP are the most efficient and effective ways to healing and both programs are offered here at Core.

Because when participating in a PHP or IOP you or your loved one spends more time in therapy (our PHP is five hours a day and our IOP is three hours a day) you can get back to living life in abundance and regain the peace that you deserve in as quickly as three months to six months, rather than years with individual counseling alone.
