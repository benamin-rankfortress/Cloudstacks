Allergies in Children - Allergies in children are a very common medical condition in which the body’s immune system overreacts to certain substances, such as pollen, dust, animal dander, food, or certain medications. The immune system mistakenly identifies these substances as dangerous and produces antibodies to fight them off. This causes the body to release histamine, a chemical that triggers an allergic reaction.

Symptoms of allergies in children can include sneezing, coughing, itchy eyes, runny nose, and skin rash. In some cases, allergies can even cause anaphylaxis, a severe and potentially life-threatening reaction.

In order to diagnose allergies in children, doctors may use a combination of physical exams, skin tests, blood tests, and allergy tests. Treatment for allergies in children may include avoiding the allergen, medications, or immunotherapy.

What Are The Symptoms Of Allergy? - Allergies can trigger a wide range of symptoms in children, and the severity can vary depending on the type of allergy. Brace yourself for the perplexing and bursty symptoms that your child may experience:

Skin rash and itchy skin: Hives are a common allergic reaction that manifests as mosquito-bite-like bumps throughout the body, causing extreme itchiness.

Breathing problems: Inflammation triggered by allergens can lead to sneezing, wheezing, shortness of breath, and narrowing of the upper airway (pharynx or trachea), which can be quite distressing.

Stomach problems: If your child has a food allergy, they may experience vomiting, nausea, and diarrhea, which can be quite jarring and unpredictable.

Circulation problems: Allergies can cause pale skin, dizziness, fainting, and a general feeling of discomfort. These symptoms can be overwhelming and require immediate medical attention.

Remember, the more parts of the body that are affected by an allergy, the more severe it is, and the quicker you need to act. If your child experiences any of these symptoms, seek medical help immediately to avoid any further complications.

What Kind of Substances Triggers Allergies? - To truly understand the complexity of what substances trigger allergies, we must delve into the intricate workings of the human immune system. The immune system is designed to protect us from harmful foreign substances, such as viruses and bacteria, but sometimes it can mistake harmless substances, like pollen or pet dander, as harmful invaders. When this happens, the immune system overreacts and produces an allergic response.

The substances that trigger allergies, also known as allergens, can vary greatly depending on the individual and their specific immune system. Common allergens include pollen, dust mites, mold spores, pet dander, certain foods like nuts or shellfish, and insect stings.

Interestingly, the severity of an allergic reaction can also vary greatly. Some people may experience mild symptoms, such as a runny nose or itchy eyes, while others may experience more severe symptoms, such as difficulty breathing or anaphylaxis, which can be life-threatening.

To avoid triggering an allergic reaction, it’s important to identify your specific allergens and take measures to minimize your exposure to them. This may involve lifestyle changes, such as avoiding certain foods or keeping pets out of the home, or medical treatment, such as allergy shots or medication.

What exactly happens during an allergic reaction? - Allergic reactions occur in two stages, with the first being infra-clinical, which is often unnoticed without any apparent symptoms. During this stage, the allergen is encountered by the immune system for the first time, and certain immune cells become sensitized and remember the allergen. When the body encounters the same allergen again, these immune cells are rapidly activated.

During the second encounter, the sensitized immune cells quickly release biologically active substances, including the essential histamine, which leads to an exaggerated histamine release that causes the symptoms associated with an allergic reaction.

In severe allergic reactions, the histamine release is excessive, resulting in not only local allergic symptoms such as redness, swelling, and itching but also systemic complications, including the dreadful state of anaphylactic shock. The histamine substance travels throughout the body and affects other organs, causing a variety of complications.
