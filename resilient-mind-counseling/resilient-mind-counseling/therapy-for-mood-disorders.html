Empowering Neurodivergent Individuals through Therapeutic Support - Introduction: Understanding Neurodivergence and the Need for Affirming Therapy:

In the vibrant tapestry of human diversity, neurodivergence stands as a testament to the unique ways our brains function and perceive the world. Neurodivergence refers to the naturally-occurring differences in brain functioning and the resulting behaviors. It is an integral part of human variation, much like differences in physical traits such as height or eye color. Yet, despite the natural occurrence of neurodivergence, society, and traditional therapeutic practices have often framed certain neurotypes as “different” or “othered,” causing undue harm and misunderstanding.

The people of Asheville, North Carolina, are no strangers to these challenges. For those who identify as neurodivergent — whether it’s ADHD, autism, learning disorders, or any mental health diagnosis — finding a therapeutic approach that respects and affirms their unique experiences can be a daunting task. Traditional therapy settings may leave them feeling misunderstood or judged, accentuating feelings of isolation and distress. There is a dire need for understanding, for empathy, and for therapists who affirm neurodivergence, seeing it not as a flaw, but as a natural variant in the human experience.

This is where neurodiversity-affirming therapy comes in, a therapeutic approach that recognizes the inherent strengths in neurodivergence and fosters them, rather than seeking to “fix” or “correct” them. With a focus on empowering neurodivergent individuals through therapeutic support, this article explores the importance of neurodiversity-affirming therapy, the role of neurodiversity-affirming therapists, and how services like those offered by Resilient Mind Counseling can make a significant difference in the lives of neurodivergent individuals.

Defining Neurodivergence: Who Counts as Neurodivergent? - Following the empowering approach to therapeutic support, it’s important to understand exactly who falls under the umbrella term “neurodivergent.” Coined by sociologist Dr. Judy Singer, the term “neurodivergence” represents the unique ways individuals perceive and interact with their surroundings. It’s a recognition that there is no one-size-fits-all definition of “normal” when it comes to brain function and behavior.

In its broadest definition, neurodivergence encompasses any divergence from what is considered “typical” or “normal” in terms of neurological development. This includes, but is not limited to, individuals diagnosed with ADHD, autism, epilepsy, and complex PTSD. It can also include those with learning disorders or intellectual disabilities. However, it’s essential to understand that not everyone who is neurodivergent considers their divergence to be a disability.

Interestingly, the term “neurodivergent” was initially meant to include all brains that diverge from neurotypical standards, even those with any mental health diagnosis. It’s a reflection of the diversity in brain function and behavior that naturally exists among humans, much like the diversity in physical characteristics like height, eye color, and family traits.

Mental health

The understanding and acceptance of neurodivergence are crucial in fostering an environment that celebrates rather than stigmatizes these differences. Identifying as neurodivergent does not indicate that something is wrong or problematic with an individual. Instead, it’s a recognition of the unique way they perceive and navigate the world around them.

In the context of therapy, understanding neurodivergence is key to providing appropriate and effective therapeutic support. By recognizing and honoring these differences, therapists can better align their approaches with the needs and experiences of neurodivergent individuals, fostering an environment of understanding, acceptance, and empowerment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I don’t want to talk about something?

If something feels overwhelming or even terrifying to discuss, that’s actually very normal. It can often feel difficult to talk about some things. During sessions, your therapist will never push you to talk about things that are too painful for you to talk about. They will be there to guide you towards discovering what feels comfortable for you to share. Working at your pace, together you can look at the tough stuff if you’re ready.

You can also share with them that you are nervous to open up about something, and you and your therapist can talk about that too. Whatever is present for you. Whatever you are going through. Bring that to session and you can explore that together.

I’m scared that if I open up, you will think I’m crazy.

If you open up, we will not think you are crazy. We will think you are someone who’s very courageous. Many clients have had the same fear (that people would think they are crazy).

Through our own life journeys and working with clients, we know how “normal” your experience is.

You’re not being dramatic or “too much.” Nothing you can say will cause us to judge you.

We know nobody’s perfect. We all struggle. We all suffer in different ways.

Whatever it is you’re going through, we provide a safe space where you can feel heard and supported without judgment.

What can I expect during the first session?

Overall, you can think of the first session as a structured assessment that helps your therapist get to know you and the challenges that brought you into treatment. They’ll invite you to share more about yourself, and you can also talk about what’s worked or not worked for your mental health in the past.

Some questions that your therapist might ask during a first session include:

  • What brought you to therapy?
  • Have you gone to therapy before? How did that go?
  • What do I need to know about you to understand your current challenges?

You can ask your therapist questions as well. You might be curious about their experience or how future sessions work.

The most important part of a first session is that you see if you feel your therapist is a good fit for you. If you feel supported, seen, and at ease with them, you can discuss if you’d like to meet again.

How can I get started?

We’re here to talk during a free phone consultation where you can tell a therapist what’s up and decide together how they can help.

Call us today to get your free phone consultation at (828) 515-1246 or Contact Us.

How long will therapy take?

You might be asking about how long therapy will take because you are currently struggling and want relief fast.

If this is true for you, we want you to know that on average, new clients start seeing small amounts of growth after the first few sessions.

By that point, they feel a little more calm and hopeful that healing is possible.

The total duration of therapy varies from client to client. Most of our clients tend to stay with us for longer than 12 months to be enough to work through the deep issues that are holding them back. While other clients may feel more supported without longer-term therapy.

We will work together to find the length of time that works for you.
